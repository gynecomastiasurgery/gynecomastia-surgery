Types of Gynecomastia

Gynecomastia is a complex condition that can manifest in different forms, each with its unique characteristics. Understanding the type of gynecomastia you have is essential in developing a personalized treatment plan that addresses your specific needs. During consultations at my practice, we perform thorough assessments to identify the type of gynecomastia you’re experiencing and recommend tailored solutions accordingly.

Glandular Gynecomastia

Glandular gynecomastia is characterized by the enlargement of glandular tissue in the male breast. This type often results in a firmer and more palpable breast tissue. It’s commonly caused by an imbalance between estrogen and testosterone levels, leading to an overgrowth of glandular tissue. Non-surgical treatments can effectively target and reduce the excess glandular tissue, providing a more masculine chest appearance.

Fatty Gynecomastia

Fatty gynecomastia, as the name suggests, involves an accumulation of excess fat in the chest area. This type may not necessarily involve glandular tissue overgrowth. It can often be attributed to factors such as genetics, hormonal imbalances, or weight fluctuations. Non-surgical treatments that focus on fat reduction can help contour the chest and create a more sculpted appearance.

Mixed Gynecomastia

Mixed gynecomastia is a combination of both glandular and fatty components. This type is relatively common and requires a comprehensive approach to address both tissue types effectively. Non-surgical treatments tailored to your unique needs can target both glandular and fatty tissue, resulting in a balanced and proportionate chest contour.

Here’s a summarized comparison of the different types of gynecomastia:

Type of Gynecomastia Characteristics Suitable Treatment Approach
Glandular Enlarged glandular tissue, firmness Non-surgical treatment targeting glandular tissue
Fatty Accumulation of excess fat Non-surgical treatment targeting fat tissue
Mixed Combination of glandular and fatty tissue Non-surgical treatment addressing both components
